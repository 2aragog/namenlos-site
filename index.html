CHAPTER 1 — FREE

The shop had no sign. No address. No name anyone remembered.

Rita found it the way most people find things they shouldn't — by not looking. By turning left when she meant to turn right, by following the alley instead of the avenue, by trusting her feet more than her phone.

The door was heavy, older than the building around it. Wood so dark it looked burned. A brass handle shaped like a hand — five fingers curled inward, as if gripping something invisible.

She pushed.

Inside: shelves. Hundreds of them. Objects she couldn't name. A kerosene lamp on the counter, the only light, and behind it — a man. Or something shaped like one.

"You're early," he said. His voice was dry leaves on stone. "Or late. I can never tell."

Rita opened her mouth to speak. But her eyes had already found it. On the center shelf, coiled like a sleeping snake: the whip.

Black leather. Braided tight. The handle was bone — not imitation, not plastic, real bone, smooth from centuries of hands.

It was the most beautiful thing she had ever seen.

"Don't," the shopkeeper said.

But Rita was already reaching.

THE GLOVE

You noticed. Most don't.

The glove lay on the lowest shelf, forgotten or deliberately hidden. Black kid leather, the left hand only, the fingers curled inward as if still gripping something.

The shopkeeper had placed it there on a Tuesday in 1987. Or 1887. Time moved differently in the shop.

A woman had come in that day, asking for something to hold. "I keep dropping everything," she said. "My keys. My coffee. My life."

He sold her the glove. She never dropped anything again. But she also couldn't let go.

She returned the glove on a Thursday. Her fingers were raw.

"Keep it," she said. "Some things hold too tight."
